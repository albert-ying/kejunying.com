Designing a Research Lab from Zero

9 February 2026

I’m starting my own lab later this year. I’ve been spending a lot of time thinking about what that should look like, and I keep coming back to one question: if you were designing an academic research lab from zero in 2025, would it look like the labs we have now?

I genuinely don’t know the answer. But I have some instincts, and I want to share them honestly, including the parts I’m uncertain about.

Over the past few years, my own research has started to change shape in ways I didn’t plan. When I built ClockBase Agent, an AI system that mines large-scale molecular profiles to discover aging interventions, it didn’t just speed up an existing workflow. It made a new kind of question tractable. We screened thousands of compounds computationally, flagged ouabain as a candidate, and then validated it experimentally: reduced frailty and improved cardiac function in aged mice. That loop, from AI-driven discovery to animal validation, would have taken a large team years using traditional approaches. It took a small group of people working closely with AI tools considerably less.

That experience changed how I think about lab structure. Not because I believe AI replaces researchers. It doesn’t. The biological reasoning, the experimental design, the judgment call about what’s real versus what’s an artifact: those remain deeply human. But a huge fraction of what fills a researcher’s day is not that. It’s literature searching, data preprocessing, pipeline debugging, figure formatting, methods writing. Skilled work, but work that AI agents are becoming genuinely good at.

The standard academic lab was designed around a constraint: human hours are scarce and expensive. One PI, five to fifteen trainees, each independently reading papers, running analyses, writing code. The PI holds the intellectual map of the whole operation, and everyone else sees their piece. Weekly lab meeting is the synchronization point. This structure works. It has produced extraordinary science for decades. I’m not dismissing it.

But I wonder whether a lab built today might look different. Not radically, alien-civilization different. More like how genomics labs changed when sequencing got cheap. When the cost of generating data dropped, the labs that thrived weren’t necessarily the biggest. They were the ones that rethought what questions became askable. I think something similar is happening with computational throughput and knowledge synthesis now that AI agents can handle those at scale.

What I’m considering, concretely: a smaller team where each person operates at a higher level of abstraction. Instead of a graduate student spending their first year learning to build bioinformatics pipelines from scratch, they’d learn to design, orchestrate, and critically evaluate analyses, with AI agents handling much of the execution. Instead of each lab member independently surveying the literature for their project, a shared knowledge layer could continuously integrate new findings and map them onto the lab’s active questions.

I find this compelling, but I want to be honest about what worries me.

First, there’s a real argument that the grunt work is where scientific intuition comes from. A student who has never manually called methylation variants may not develop the instinct for when a result looks wrong. Surgeons don’t skip anatomy because they’ll eventually use robots. I don’t have a clean answer for this. My best guess is that the learning path changes rather than disappears: you still need deep understanding of the methods, but you might acquire it through designing and troubleshooting agent-run analyses rather than executing every step by hand. Whether that’s actually true is something I’ll have to learn by trying it.

Second, a smaller team sacrifices things that matter. A twenty-person lab has intellectual diversity, redundancy, and serendipitous hallway interactions that four people can’t replicate no matter how many agents they deploy. The best scientific ideas often come from a postdoc arguing with another postdoc about something unrelated to either of their projects. I don’t want to optimize for throughput and lose that. The honest answer is that I don’t yet know the right ratio.

Third, the incentive structure of academia hasn’t caught up. Tenure committees count papers, evaluate grant funding, and assess mentorship track records. If I spend my first two years building agent infrastructure and produce fewer publications than the new hire across the hall who ran a conventional operation, the long-term bet won’t matter much at the review. I’m aware of this tradeoff. My hope is that the platforms I’ve already built (ClockBase, MethylGPT, Biolearn) provide enough of a foundation that the infrastructure phase is shorter than it would be for someone starting cold. But hope is not a plan, and I’m still working on making the numbers work.

I also want to be clear: I don’t think established labs are stuck. Many of the best computational biology groups have reinvented their approaches repeatedly. The advantage of starting fresh isn’t that incumbents are incapable. It’s simpler than that: when you have no existing workflows, you have no switching costs. That’s a small, temporary edge, not a permanent one.

So where does this leave me? With a bet, not a blueprint. I believe that a thoughtfully designed small lab, with AI agents woven into its daily operation, can produce science that competes with much larger groups. I believe the training model can evolve so that students learn at a higher level without losing rigor. I believe the tools are ready enough to try this now, though not so mature that success is guaranteed.

I could be wrong about all of this. The only way to find out is to build it and see. That’s what I plan to do.
